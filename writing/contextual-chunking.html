On contextual chunking: how we cut RAG retrieval errors by 62%

Naive chunking throws away the document's structure — and most retrieval failures we saw in production at Crédit Agricole came from chunks that were technically relevant but contextually orphaned. Here's how prepending hierarchical context changed the math.

The problem with chunking

[Draft note: open with the concrete failure mode you saw. A user query that should have returned a clear answer, a chunk that was technically relevant, but the model couldn't ground it without surrounding context. This is your hook.]

When you split a document for embedding, you're making an implicit bet: that each chunk carries enough self-contained meaning to be retrieved on its own merits. For a lot of documents — short FAQs, articles with self-explanatory paragraphs — that bet pays off. For dense, hierarchical, internal documents (regulatory texts, technical specs, banking policies), it falls apart.

A chunk that says "this category is excluded from the calculation" is technically meaningful, but it's useless on retrieval — it doesn't tell you which category, which calculation, or which policy. Embedding it plus its surrounding context separately means the chunk's vector lives somewhere far from where its meaning actually points.

What we built

The fix is mechanically simple: before embedding each chunk, prepend a short, structured summary of where it sits in the document hierarchy. Section title, parent section, document title, sometimes the immediately preceding paragraph's topic.

[Draft note: insert your concrete schema here. Something like:] 

// Each chunk before embedding:
{
  "context": "Document: Risk Policy v3.2 / Section 4.1: Exclusions / Subsection: Retail",
  "content": "[the actual chunk text]"
}

The embedding then sits in semantic space at a coordinate that reflects both what the chunk says and where it lives. A query about "retail risk exclusions" now retrieves a chunk whose vector knows it's about retail risk exclusions, even when the chunk itself never says those words.

Implementation tradeoffs

[Draft note: discuss the tradeoffs you actually faced — token cost of context prepending, how much context is too much, whether to use the LLM to generate the context summary or rely on document structure parsers, etc.]

  • Context length — too little and you don't disambiguate; too much and you dilute the chunk's signal.
  • Context generation — manual structural parsing is cheap and deterministic; LLM-generated context is richer but introduces latency and a quality dependency.
  • Hybrid retrieval — we paired this with BM25 indexing on the contextualized chunk to keep keyword precision.

What I'd do differently

[Draft note: this is where you separate the post from a marketing piece — be honest about what didn't work, what you'd skip in v2, what you'd push further. The reader wants to know you've thought past the headline.]

In retrospect, the biggest win wasn't the chunking strategy itself — it was building the eval pipeline first. Without DeepEval and RAGAS scoring every retrieval change against a frozen test set, we'd have shipped half a dozen subjectively "better" chunkers and chased phantom regressions. Instrument before you optimize.
